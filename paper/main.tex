\begin{abstract}
Evaluating recommendation explanations typically relies on human studies, which tend to be expensive and hard to repeat at scale. In this paper, we present a student-scale implementation of a persona-conditioned evaluation pipeline that brings together data-driven user archetypes, calibrated synthetic panel judgments, and benchmark-based analysis. Starting from MovieLens 10M, we cluster users into five behavioral archetypes, build trait-based persona prompts, generate feature, neighbor, and counterfactual explanations, and evaluate a stratified sample along four dimensions: utility, trust, persuasiveness, and cognitive load. The results we report come from the repository's local calibrated evaluation path rather than a live multi-agent LLM debate. On the saved pipeline outputs, the system achieves a Human Alignment Score of 0.8879, preserves the feature $>$ neighbor $>$ counterfactual ranking, and passes the adversarial disconfirmation gate (DS = 1.25 $\leq$ 2.0). We also find a statistically significant cognitive-load difference between Casual Positive Raters and Critical Analysts ($d$ = $-$0.846, $p$ = 0.0031). We see these findings as evidence that the pipeline works well enough for controlled experimentation, though we do not claim it can replace human evaluation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Recommendation explanations serve several purposes: they help users make informed decisions, build trust in algorithmic systems, and increase the persuasiveness of suggestions~\cite{tintarev2007survey}. Tintarev and Masthoff's well-known taxonomy identifies seven explanation aims---transparency, scrutability, trust, effectiveness, persuasiveness, efficiency, and satisfaction---which together define what a ``good'' explanation should achieve. Yet evaluating whether explanations meet these aims remains a persistent bottleneck. Human user studies provide high ecological validity, but they are expensive, slow, and hard to scale across the combinatorial space of explanation types, user populations, and evaluation dimensions~\cite{kouki2019}.

Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have made automated evaluation more feasible. LLMs can follow persona instructions and produce structured judgments, making them attractive as a faster evaluation tool~\cite{argyle2023}. At the same time, LLM-based evaluation raises clear concerns: ratings can skew too positive, different perspectives can collapse into a single voice, and apparent agreement with human judgments may partly reflect prompt design rather than genuine behavioral fidelity~\cite{zheng2023judging}. A single evaluator is especially limited when the goal is to understand how different kinds of users react to the same explanation.

We address these challenges with a persona-conditioned evaluation framework and a working end-to-end prototype. Our approach makes three main contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Data-driven persona construction.} We cluster 43,608 users from MovieLens 10M into five behavioral archetypes using K-Means on engineered features (genre preferences, rating patterns, diversity indices) and then map each cluster to a psychographic persona prompt that captures cognitive style and interaction goals.

\item \textbf{Persona-conditioned panel simulation.} Each explanation is evaluated by a three-agent panel that shares an archetype but differs in personality quirks (skeptical, concise-preferring, emotion-oriented), which gives us within-panel variance for comparative analysis. An optional live evaluation path exists in the repository, but we did not use it for the final saved experiment.

\item \textbf{Validation against human benchmarks.} We introduce the Literature-Derived Evaluation Scenarios (LiDES) benchmark, which codifies quantitative findings from Kouki et al.'s~\cite{kouki2019} Amazon Mechanical Turk study ($N$=198) and lets us systematically compare LLM panel judgments to human evaluations.
\end{enumerate}

We investigate the following research questions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{RQ1:} Do persona-conditioned evaluators give meaningfully different judgments depending on the user archetype?
\item \textbf{RQ2:} Does a panel-based evaluation design offer a useful alternative to single-perspective evaluation?
\item \textbf{RQ3:} Do different explanation types lead to different cognitive-load patterns across archetypes, particularly for analytically demanding explanations like counterfactuals?
\end{itemize}

Our framework evaluates three explanation types---feature-based (content similarity), neighbor-based (social proof), and counterfactual (causal reasoning)---across four dimensions: utility, trust, persuasiveness, and cognitive load. The prototype shows reasonable benchmark alignment and meaningful differences between archetypes, suggesting that persona-conditioned evaluation can be useful as a development tool, though it still needs direct human validation.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Recommendation Explanations}

The design space for recommendation explanations has been studied extensively. Tintarev and Masthoff~\cite{tintarev2007survey} provide a foundational survey that organizes explanation research around seven aims. Their framework shows that different explanation types serve different purposes: content-based explanations support transparency, while collaborative filtering explanations rely on social proof for persuasiveness.

Kouki et al.~\cite{kouki2019} conducted one of the most comprehensive empirical studies on explanation preferences, comparing seven explanation styles in a hybrid recommender system via an Amazon Mechanical Turk study with 198 participants. Their key findings include: (1) item-based collaborative filtering explanations received the highest persuasiveness ratings ($M$ = 5.3 on a 7-point scale), (2) content-based explanations (Jaccard similarity) were close behind ($M$ = 5.2), and (3) text-based explanation formats significantly outperformed visual formats ($F$ = 10.13, $p$ < 0.001). They also found that personality traits (Big Five, Need for Cognition) moderated explanation preferences, which motivated our persona-conditioned approach.

\subsection{LLMs as Evaluators}

Using LLMs as automated evaluators has been gaining traction across NLP tasks. Zheng et al.~\cite{zheng2023judging} introduced MT-Bench and showed that GPT-4 judgments correlate with human preferences at rates comparable to inter-human agreement. However, they also documented systematic biases, including position bias and verbosity preference.

Argyle et al.~\cite{argyle2023} demonstrated that LLMs can simulate survey-style responses that reproduce some known social-science patterns, which provides motivation for persona-based evaluation. We use this line of work as background motivation; our own results should be read as pipeline outputs rather than evidence that the simulated personas behave like real users.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Evaluation}

Multi-agent approaches to LLM evaluation are still relatively underexplored. Chan et al.~\cite{chan2023chateval} proposed ChatEval, where multiple LLM agents engage in debate to produce more robust text quality assessments. Our work borrows the idea of using multiple evaluators, though the experiment we report uses the repository's calibrated local panel generator rather than a full live debate.

In the context of this assignment, the main contribution is not a new production system but rather a working pipeline that brings together archetype construction, explanation generation, persona-conditioned scoring, benchmark comparison, and adversarial probes in one reproducible workflow.

\subsection{Research Gap and Originality}

Taken together, the literature points to three unresolved issues. First, explanation studies in recommender systems tell us what kinds of explanations users tend to prefer, but they do not offer a cheap way to test many explanation variants across many user types~\cite{tintarev2007survey,kouki2019}. Second, LLM-as-a-judge work shows that automated evaluators can correlate with human judgments, but it typically relies on a single evaluator perspective rather than explicitly modeling heterogeneous users~\cite{zheng2023judging}. Third, multi-agent LLM evaluation introduces perspective diversity, but prior work has not focused on recommendation explanations or data-driven user archetypes~\cite{chan2023chateval}. Our contribution therefore lies in the combination: a working, student-scale pipeline that starts from clustered recommender users, constructs archetype prompts, evaluates multiple explanation families with a small panel, and checks the outputs against both benchmark scenarios and adversarial probes.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} illustrates our six-stage pipeline. We describe each stage below.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing (Stage 1)}

We use the MovieLens 10M dataset~\cite{harper2015movielens}, which contains 10,000,054 ratings from 69,878 users on 10,677 movies. We apply two filters: users must have at least 50 ratings to ensure sufficient behavioral signal, and items must have at least 10 ratings for adequate community feedback. After filtering, this leaves 9,164,602 ratings from 43,608 users on 9,708 movies.

\subsection{Feature Engineering and Clustering (Stage 2)}

For each user, we compute five features: (1) mean rating $\mu_r$, (2) rating standard deviation $\sigma_r$, (3) total rating count $n_r$, (4) a genre proportion vector $\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{R}^{18}$ (the proportion of rated movies in each of the 18 MovieLens genres), and (5) the Inverse Simpson Index (ISI), which measures genre diversity:

\begin{equation}
\text{ISI} = \frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^{G} p_i^2}
\end{equation}

where $p_i$ is the proportion of a user's ratings in genre $i$. Higher ISI indicates more diverse viewing patterns.

We then run K-Means clustering ($k$=5, chosen to match our five-archetype design) on standardized features, which gives us the archetypes shown in Table~\ref{tab:archetypes}.

\midrule

The largest cluster (Critical Analyst, $n$=16,928) has the lowest mean rating and moderate diversity, while Casual Positive Raters show the highest mean rating (3.78) with the lowest standard deviation (0.92). Niche Explorers have the highest ISI (10.22), reflecting their broad genre exploration.

\subsection{Persona Construction (Stage 3)}

Each archetype is mapped to a psychographic persona prompt following Strategy A (trait-only), which specifies:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Trait description}: Behavioral patterns grounded in cluster statistics (e.g., ``You tend to rate most movies positively, averaging around 3.8/5'').
\item \textbf{Cognitive style}: Need for cognition level and information processing preference.
\item \textbf{Interaction goal}: What the user seeks from recommendations.
\end{itemize}

We deliberately exclude demographic attributes (age, gender, occupation) in Strategy A to isolate the effect of psychographic traits on evaluation behavior, avoiding the demographic bias concerns raised by Argyle et al.~\cite{argyle2023}. Strategy B (demographic augmentation) is implemented and was run as a secondary condition within the methodology experiments, but we did not use it as the primary reported condition. We discuss it as future work for a more thorough investigation.

\subsection{Explanation Generation (Stage 4)}

For each of 500 seed users (100 per archetype, selected as closest to cluster centroids), we generate three explanation types for a recommended movie:

\textbf{Feature-based}: ``Based on your high ratings for `Toy Story' and `Casino', we believe you'll enjoy `Enchanted' because it shares the Comedy and Musical elements you appreciate.''

\textbf{Neighbor-based}: ``Users with similar taste to yours also rated `Enchanted' highly. 7 users who share your preference pattern gave this movie an average rating of 4.4 out of 5.''

\textbf{Counterfactual}: ``We recommend `Enchanted' primarily because you rated `Toy Story' highly (5.0/5). If you had rated it below 3, this movie would not appear in your recommendations.''

This yields 1,500 total evaluation tasks. In the implementation, feature-based and counterfactual explanations are grounded in user history, while neighbor-based explanations use a lightweight template proxy rather than a full collaborative filtering similarity computation. We then sample 45 tasks (9 per archetype, stratified across explanation types) for the primary evaluation, plus 12 adversarial probes.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Panel Evaluation (Stage 5)}

Each sampled task is evaluated by a panel of three agents. All agents share the same archetype persona but differ in a personality quirk:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item Agent 1: Slightly skeptical of AI-generated recommendations
\item Agent 2: Prefers concise, bullet-point style communication
\item Agent 3: Values emotional resonance over pure logic
\end{itemize}

Each agent rates the explanation on four dimensions using calibrated Likert scales:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Utility} (1--5): How useful is this explanation for deciding whether to watch the movie?
\item \textbf{Trust} (1--5): How much does this explanation make you trust the recommender system?
\item \textbf{Persuasiveness} (1--5): How much does this explanation make you want to watch the movie?
\item \textbf{Cognitive Load} (1--7): How much mental effort was required to process this explanation?
\end{itemize}

Calibration examples anchor the scale: a vague explanation (``Based on AI analysis, this is a great fit'') should receive 1--2, a generic explanation (``You liked Action, here's more Action'') should receive 3, and a specific personalized explanation should receive 4--5.

In the original methodology, these panel members were intended to participate in a moderator-led debate and revise their scores. That live deliberation pipeline was implemented as a separate code path, but the final saved experiment reported in this paper uses a calibrated simulation layer that generates panel-member scores and fixed justification templates under the configured archetype and quirk settings. Accordingly, our claims in the Results and Discussion sections are limited to what the implemented prototype can demonstrate, rather than a fully executed live debate study.

\subsection{Adversarial Disconfirmation Probes}

To verify that personas can detect low-quality explanations, we include 40 deliberately flawed explanations in four categories:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Logical contradiction}: Claims preferences the Critical Analyst persona does not hold (``you prefer lighthearted family comedies'').
\item \textbf{Irrelevant reasoning}: Cites metadata with no preference relevance (``runtime of 148 minutes, released on a Tuesday'').
\item \textbf{Factual fabrication}: Attributes false cast/director information.
\item \textbf{Empty circular}: Uses impressive-sounding but vacuous language (``advanced AI analysis of your unique profile'').
\end{enumerate}

A subset of 12 adversarial probes (3 per category) are evaluated by Critical Analyst panels. The framework passes the disconfirmation gate if the mean adversarial score is $\leq$ 2.0.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{LiDES Benchmark}

We introduce the \textbf{Literature-Derived Evaluation Scenarios (LiDES)} benchmark (v2.0), which codifies 18 evaluation scenarios based on published human study data. The main source is Kouki et al.~\cite{kouki2019}, whose Amazon Mechanical Turk study ($N$=198) provides 7-point Likert ratings for seven explanation styles. We normalize these to our 5-point scale with a linear transformation:

\begin{equation}
s_{5} = \frac{s_{7} - 1}{6} \times 4 + 1
\end{equation}

Key human baselines from Kouki et al. (normalized to 5-point):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item Content-based (Jaccard): $M$ = 3.80
\item Item-based CF: $M$ = 3.67
\item User-based CF: $M$ = 3.53
\item Social-based: $M$ = 3.33
\end{itemize}

We map our explanation types to Kouki's as follows: feature-based $\approx$ content-based, neighbor-based $\approx$ user-based/social-based. Counterfactual explanations were not tested in Kouki's study, so our evaluation of this type is a novel addition.

\subsection{Metrics}

\textbf{Human Alignment Score (HAS):} Measures the mean absolute error between panel means and human study means, normalized to [0,1]:

\begin{equation}
\text{HAS} = 1 - \frac{\text{MAE}}{s_{\max} - s_{\min}}
\end{equation}

where MAE is computed per dimension across explanation types.

\textbf{Spearman Rank Correlation ($\rho$):} Tests whether panel outputs rank explanation types in the same order as humans.

\textbf{Krippendorff's Alpha ($\alpha$):} Measures inter-agent reliability within panels. Values $\geq$ 0.667 indicate acceptable agreement for exploratory research~\cite{krippendorff2011}.

\textbf{Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC$_{2,k}$):} Complementary reliability metric assessing consistency of panel ratings.

\textbf{Disconfirmation Score (DS):} Mean evaluation score on adversarial probes. Must be $\leq$ 2.0 to pass the quality gate.

\textbf{Cognitive Load Differential:} Cohen's $d$ between Critical Analyst and Casual Positive Rater archetypes on the cognitive load dimension, which tests whether personas lead to meaningfully different information processing behaviors.

\subsection{Implementation}

The pipeline is implemented in Python, using pandas and NumPy for data handling, scikit-learn for clustering, SciPy for statistical tests, and Matplotlib/Seaborn for visualization. The repository has two evaluation paths: (1) an optional live OpenAI-compatible evaluation path, and (2) a calibrated local evaluation generator. The live path works through the OpenAI Python client against a configurable OpenAI-compatible endpoint, with \texttt{nvidia/nemotron-3-super-120b-a12b} as the default configured model and optional Anthropic support scaffolded in the code. The final saved experiment we use in this paper follows the second path, which produces archetype-conditioned panel scores and justification templates without any external API calls. The live path is still part of the project artifact, but it was not the source of the reported results.

The implementation tests the three research questions as follows. For \textbf{RQ1}, we compare explanation ratings across the five archetypes under the same recommendation tasks. For \textbf{RQ2}, we compare the primary three-agent configuration against precomputed zero-shot, single-agent, heuristic, and misaligned-persona baselines. For \textbf{RQ3}, we run a proxy cognitive-load analysis, paying particular attention to counterfactual explanations and the difference between Critical Analysts and Casual Positive Raters. Across the main experiment, the evaluated dimensions are utility, trust, persuasiveness, and cognitive load; the lightweight 10-user package uses utility, trust, and persuasiveness for the teammate-facing comparison sheet.

\subsection{Saved Run Configuration}

For reproducibility, we report the exact configuration used by the saved local run. The central configuration file fixes the random seed at 42, uses a minimum of 50 ratings per user and 10 ratings per item, and clusters users into five archetypes. The run uses three explanation types (feature, neighbor, counterfactual), a three-agent panel, one moderation round, Strategy A trait-only personas, thick persona prompts, and calibrated scoring anchors. The main evaluation batch has 45 tasks, produced by sampling three tasks for each archetype--explanation-type cell, and the adversarial batch has 12 probes. The implementation also applies a movie-year cutoff at 2010 to reduce leakage from titles that might fall outside model knowledge assumptions. All reported outputs can be regenerated from the repository by running \texttt{python run\_pipeline.py} after installing the packages listed in \texttt{requirements.txt}. Since the reported run uses the local calibrated path, no GPU or paid API access is needed to reproduce the main results.

\subsection{Lightweight 10-User Validation Package}

Since the full pipeline is broader than what a small team can manually inspect in a few days, we added a lightweight validation layer on top of the main implementation. This study package samples 10 real users from the existing seed-user pool, with 2 users from each archetype. For each user, the repository generates a short persona summary, selects or constructs 5 explanation cases, and produces local evaluator ratings for utility, trust, and persuasiveness. This yields 50 total cases.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Overall Evaluation Scores}

Across the 45 main evaluation tasks, the mean score for utility, trust, and persuasiveness is $M$ = 3.16 ($SD$ = 0.78) on the 1--5 Likert scale. In practical terms, the calibrated setup does not collapse into uniformly high ratings. The distribution in Figure~\ref{fig:distribution} still skews somewhat positive, but it covers a wider portion of the scale than a naive always-positive evaluator would.

\subsection{Human Alignment}

The framework achieves an overall Human Alignment Score of \textbf{HAS = 0.8879}. In the saved metrics file, HAS is reported for the persuasiveness benchmark comparison used in LiDES. Within the scope of this implementation, the result indicates that the calibrated setup reproduces the benchmark ordering reasonably well. It should not be interpreted as direct evidence that a live LLM panel would achieve the same alignment.

\subsection{Inter-Agent Reliability}

Krippendorff's $\alpha$ = 0.6313 and ICC = 0.6414 indicate moderate agreement within panels. The per-dimension alphas are 0.6541 for utility, 0.6464 for trust, and 0.5935 for persuasiveness.

This level of agreement is adequate for the current prototype. The agents are not identical, but they are also not so unstable that the panel collapses into noise. Persuasiveness shows the lowest agreement of the three reported dimensions, which is expected since it depends more heavily on the persona's taste assumptions than utility does.

\subsection{Adversarial Disconfirmation}

The Disconfirmation Score is \textbf{DS = 1.25}, comfortably below the 2.0 threshold. The per-dimension adversarial scores are 1.333 for utility, 1.139 for trust, and 1.278 for persuasiveness. In other words, the pipeline clearly separates ordinary explanations from deliberately flawed ones.

\subsection{Cognitive Load Differential}

The cognitive load analysis shows a significant difference between archetypes. Critical Analysts report lower cognitive load ($M$ = 2.667) than Casual Positive Raters ($M$ = 3.519), $t$ = $-$3.1078, $p$ = 0.0031, Cohen's $d$ = $-$0.846.

This pattern is consistent with how the personas were designed. The Critical Analyst prompt emphasizes more deliberate comparison and reasoning, while the Casual Positive Rater prompt assumes a lower tolerance for detailed justification.

The effect size is substantial enough to suggest that persona conditioning affects more than surface-level sentiment.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Blockbuster Followers} assign relatively high persuasiveness scores when explanations reference other users, consistent with the social-proof orientation of that persona.
\item \textbf{Niche Explorers} remain less persuaded overall, especially when explanations lean on group behavior rather than individual taste.
\item \textbf{Critical Analysts} rate counterfactual explanations more harshly on trust and utility than feature-based ones, but their cognitive-load scores suggest they handle this explanation style more comfortably than Casual Positive Raters do.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baselines and Sensitivity Checks}

The repository also stores baseline comparisons and a small ablation summary, both useful for interpreting the main results more conservatively. In the saved metrics report, the multi-agent configuration outperforms the zero-shot baseline by 0.091 mean-score points, the single-agent baseline by 0.062, and the heuristic baseline by 0.048. The margin over the misaligned-persona baseline is small (0.010), which suggests that prompt structure and calibration are still driving a substantial part of the behavior.

The ablation summary points in the same direction. Neutral calibration inflates scores compared to the calibrated setup, while thicker personas and one round of moderation reduce within-panel variance relative to thinner personas or no debate. These checks are only small-scale ($n=5$ tasks per condition in the summary file), so they should be seen as directional evidence rather than definitive parameter tuning. Even so, they improve transparency by showing that the reported behavior is not completely insensitive to design choices.

\subsection{Cross-Model Validation}
\label{sec:crossmodel}

To assess whether the evaluation patterns hold across different LLMs, we ran a trimmed 19-task subset (15 main + 4 adversarial, covering all 5 archetypes $\times$ 3 explanation types and 4 adversarial flaw categories) on three models: ChatGPT~5.4, Claude Sonnet~4.6, and MiniMax~M2.7. Each model received the same one-shot prompt and returned a 3-agent panel evaluation for all 19 tasks.

\textbf{Agreement.} The cross-model ICC(2,$k$) on the evaluation dimensions (utility, trust, persuasiveness) is 0.609, indicating moderate inter-model agreement. Pairwise Spearman correlations on task-level mean scores show strong alignment between ChatGPT and Claude ($\rho = 0.93$, $p < 0.001$), with MiniMax showing moderate correlation to both ($\rho = 0.51$ and $\rho = 0.41$, respectively).

\textbf{Score distributions.} All three models produce overall means in the 2.5--2.7 range on the 1--5 scale, well below the midpoint ceiling that would signal positivity bias. MiniMax exhibits the narrowest score variance (SD = 0.63), while ChatGPT shows the widest (SD = 1.02). All three models assign the lowest scores to feature-based explanations, while neighbor-based and counterfactual explanations compete for the top position (ChatGPT ranks counterfactual highest; Claude and MiniMax rank neighbor highest).

\textbf{Adversarial robustness.} All three models unanimously pass the disconfirmation gate with a mean adversarial score of 1.0 across utility, trust, and persuasiveness, confirming that the persona-conditioned prompt structure reliably detects flawed explanations regardless of the underlying LLM.

\subsection{10-User Study Package Outputs}

The lightweight 10-user package has already been generated from the repository. It contains 10 users balanced across the five archetypes, and 50 explanation cases in total. The local LLM-side ratings are available for all cases, while the human side is intentionally left as a manual rating sheet for teammates to complete.

Even before the human ratings are added, this package is useful because it shows how the simplified study idea can be grounded in the actual implementation rather than being separate from it. Across the 50 generated cases, the local evaluator assigns the highest mean utility to the preference-summary template (3.7), the highest mean trust to the feature template (3.2), and the highest mean persuasiveness to the neighbor template (3.5). Counterfactual cases remain harder on trust (2.7) than on utility or persuasiveness (both 3.1), which is broadly in line with the larger pipeline.

We do not report a human-vs-LLM agreement result here because that comparison depends on teammate ratings that are external to the saved repository outputs. Instead, we include this package as a reproducible bridge between the full pipeline and the simplified validation design discussed within the team.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Framework Validity}

The HAS of 0.8879 shows that the prototype can be tuned to approximate the benchmark pattern used in LiDES. This does not mean the pipeline reproduces human judgment in general, and it does not validate a live multi-agent debate setup. What it does show is that the full workflow---from archetype creation to benchmark comparison---is operational and can support controlled experimentation.

The successful adversarial disconfirmation (DS = 1.25) matters for a simpler reason: the pipeline does not reward text simply because it resembles an explanation. It maintains a clear separation between ordinary and deliberately flawed inputs, which is necessary if the framework is to be useful as a debugging signal.

Compared with Kouki et al.~\cite{kouki2019}, our results should be read as pattern-level rather than score-level agreement. Feature-based explanations remain strongest on utility and trust, which is compatible with the broader literature on explanation usefulness. However, our persuasiveness ordering does not reproduce Kouki's exact ranking because neighbor-based explanations slightly exceed feature-based ones in the saved run. This difference is important to note because it shows the benchmark alignment is partial, not complete.

\subsection{The Role of Archetype Diversity}

The 1.00-point mean score spread across archetypes (from Casual Positive Rater at 3.65 to Critical Analyst at 2.65) suggests that persona conditioning produces more than superficial label effects. The interaction patterns are not dramatic, but they are directionally consistent with how the personas were constructed.

At the same time, the rank-alignment results suggest that the underlying explanation-quality signal still dominates many of the archetype differences. We do not see strong ranking reversals that would clearly separate one persona from another. For this assignment, that is acceptable, but it also suggests that richer prompts or stronger task variation may be needed if the goal is to model sharper differences in user preference.

\subsection{Inter-Agent Agreement: Feature or Bug?}

The moderate Krippendorff agreement deserves a balanced reading. Some disagreement is desirable because the agent quirks are intended to create perspective diversity, but the current values are still below what we would want from a mature evaluation instrument~\cite{krippendorff2011}. In this prototype, the panel is stable enough to be informative but not strong enough to stand in for human agreement studies.

The key observation is that the \textit{consensus} (panel mean) is more stable than individual agent scores, analogous to how survey research relies on aggregate trends rather than individual responses.

\subsection{Counterfactual Explanations: A Novel Contribution}

Neither Tintarev and Masthoff~\cite{tintarev2007survey} nor Kouki et al.~\cite{kouki2019} focused their evaluations on counterfactual explanations. In our saved run, counterfactual explanations score lowest on utility and trust but highest on cognitive load. This provides at least an initial view of how this explanation type behaves within the pipeline.

The mean cognitive-load scores from the saved run follow the same pattern: counterfactual explanations average 4.07, compared to 2.71 for feature-based and 2.62 for neighbor-based explanations. This suggests that counterfactual reasoning may offer transparency at a noticeable processing cost, especially for lower-engagement personas.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

Even in prototype form, our framework enables several practical applications:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Rapid prototyping}: Developers can evaluate explanation candidates across five user archetypes without recruiting participants, enabling faster iteration cycles.
\item \textbf{Coverage testing}: The combinatorial space of explanation types $\times$ user archetypes $\times$ evaluation dimensions is too large for any single human study. Persona-conditioned panels can provide broad coverage to identify promising directions before investing in targeted human validation.
\item \textbf{Regression testing}: When updating explanation generation logic, the framework can serve as an automated regression test ensuring that changes do not degrade the experience for any archetype.
\end{enumerate}

These implications should still be read within the limits of the current implementation. The framework is most defensible as an internal comparison tool for explanation variants, not as a substitute for participant studies or as evidence of real user welfare effects.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

\subsection{Construct Validity}

\textbf{Persona fidelity.} Persona-conditioned evaluators may not faithfully represent the cognitive processes of real users. While our personas are grounded in behavioral data (cluster statistics), the mapping from cluster centroids to psychographic descriptions involves researcher judgment. The exclusion of demographics in Strategy A reduces bias risk but may miss personality--demographic interactions documented by Kouki et al.~\cite{kouki2019}.

\textbf{Evaluation dimensions.} Our four-dimension evaluation (utility, trust, persuasiveness, cognitive load) covers the most commonly studied dimensions but does not capture all of Tintarev and Masthoff's seven aims. Scrutability and effectiveness would require interactive evaluation setups that go beyond single-turn ratings.

\subsection{Internal Validity}

\textbf{Implementation gap.} The final saved experiment is not the full methodology we originally proposed in Task 2. Strategy B, temperature sweeps, and ablation studies are implemented and executed in the codebase, but only at small scale within the methodology experiments and ablation stages---they are not the primary reported conditions. Live moderator-led debate was implemented as a separate code path but was not used for the final saved results. A cross-model validation was conducted on a trimmed 19-task subset across three LLMs (Section~\ref{sec:crossmodel}), yielding moderate agreement (ICC = 0.609), but this only covers a fraction of the full experimental matrix. This constrains how strongly we can answer RQ2.

\textbf{LLM sensitivity.} Results may vary across LLM versions, temperatures, and prompting strategies. We use calibration examples to stabilize ratings, but the specific calibration anchors may introduce subtle biases. Although the repository contains a live LLM evaluation path, the final reported results come from a calibrated synthetic generator, so sensitivity to live model behavior remains untested.

\textbf{Sample size.} Our primary evaluation uses 45 tasks (9 per archetype). While stratified sampling ensures coverage, this is insufficient for fine-grained statistical comparisons within archetype--explanation type cells ($n$ = 3 per cell). The rank correlation results ($\rho$ = 0.50 with $p$ = 0.67) reflect this limitation.

\textbf{Parameter dependence.} The ablation summary shows that calibration, persona granularity, and moderation choices can all shift mean scores and panel variance. This is useful for diagnostics, but it also means the results are partly dependent on prompt settings chosen by the researchers rather than purely on explanation quality.

\textbf{Mini-study incompleteness.} The lightweight 10-user package is fully generated, but the final human-vs-LLM comparison still depends on teammate ratings that are not included in the repository by default. Consequently, the package strengthens transparency and practical usability, but it should not be presented as completed human validation unless those ratings are actually collected and analyzed.

\subsection{External Validity}

\textbf{Domain specificity.} Our framework is evaluated exclusively on movie recommendations using MovieLens data. Generalizability to other domains (e-commerce, music, news) requires further validation.

\textbf{Benchmark limitations.} The LiDES benchmark relies mainly on a small set of literature-derived scenarios, with its central grounding coming from Kouki et al.~\cite{kouki2019}. While this supports a structured benchmark, it is still a ``silver standard'' rather than direct human labels collected for the exact tasks in this study.

\textbf{Temporal validity.} Both the MovieLens 10M data and Kouki et al.'s study predate the current generation of LLMs. User expectations for explanation quality may have shifted since then.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a persona-conditioned framework and a working prototype for evaluating recommendation explanations using data-driven user archetypes. Our approach bridges the gap between expensive human studies and simplistic automated metrics by introducing psychographically grounded evaluation panels and a benchmark-aware analysis workflow.

Our key findings:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item The prototype reaches HAS = 0.8879 on the saved benchmark comparison, showing that the calibrated pipeline can approximate the target ranking used in LiDES.
\item Persona conditioning produces a 1.00-point spread between the highest- and lowest-scoring archetypes, indicating that the prompts produce more than cosmetic label effects.
\item The adversarial disconfirmation gate (DS = 1.25) confirms that the pipeline can distinguish ordinary explanations from intentionally flawed ones.
\item Counterfactual explanations receive the highest cognitive-load scores in the saved run, while feature-based explanations remain the strongest overall on utility and trust.
\item A cross-model validation on a trimmed 19-task subset across ChatGPT~5.4, Claude Sonnet~4.6, and MiniMax~M2.7 yields moderate inter-model agreement (ICC = 0.609) and unanimous adversarial detection (DS = 1.0 across all models), providing initial evidence for cross-model robustness.
\item A lightweight 10-user validation package has been added on top of the main pipeline, providing the team with a concrete path to run a simpler human-vs-LLM comparison without rewriting the methodology from scratch.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Future work.} Four directions merit investigation: (1) validating the framework with a dedicated human study that directly compares panel ratings to matched human participants on the same tasks, (2) scaling the cross-model validation to the full 57-task matrix and additional LLM families, (3) extending to multi-turn interactive evaluation where users can ask follow-up questions about explanations, and (4) exploring Strategy B (demographic-augmented) personas to study how demographic conditioning affects evaluation bias and alignment.

The framework's main value is as a \textit{complement} to human evaluation, not a replacement for it. By enabling rapid, scalable, and diverse evaluation of recommendation explanations, persona-conditioned LLM panels can accelerate the development cycle while preserving the nuance of human-centered evaluation.
